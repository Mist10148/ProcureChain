SENTINEL: Reflecting on the Future of Computing Through Predictive Disaster 
Warning Technology 
The Philippines is no stranger to natural disasters. Every year, the country faces an average 
of twenty typhoons, sits directly on the Pacific Ring of Fire, and deals with constant threats 
from volcanic eruptions, earthquakes, and flash floods. Growing up here, it almost feels routine 
to see news coverage of communities being devastated by a storm surge or a landslide. But 
the more you think about it, the more it becomes clear that many of those casualties did not 
have to happen. The problem is rarely just the disaster itself. More often, it is the fact that 
people did not know it was coming until it was already too late to do anything about it. 
This is the problem that got me thinking about what the future of computing could realistically 
look like. Existing warning systems in the country, while functional, still carry a significant gap 
between detection and delivery. PAGASA and PHIVOLCS monitor seismic and atmospheric 
activity, but that information does not always reach ordinary citizens fast enough to be 
actionable. A typhoon bulletin broadcast on the radio does not help a farming family in a remote 
barangay with no signal. A text alert sent thirty minutes after shaking has already started is not 
really a warning anymore. The core issue is the delay between when a disaster begins forming 
and when the people most at risk are actually told to move. 
From this problem, I imagined a system I would call SENTINEL, which stands for Smart 
Environmental Network for Threat Intelligence and Natural Emergency Localized Intervention. 
The main objective of this concept is to design a predictive early warning platform that detects 
the early signs of natural disasters in the moments before they fully materialize, and delivers 
localized alerts to communities in real time. More specifically, the system would aim to monitor 
seismic, atmospheric, and hydrological conditions simultaneously, use a machine learning 
model trained on historical disaster data to identify precursor patterns, and push warnings 
through multiple channels including SMS and community-level audio alerts, even in areas with 
limited connectivity. 
The idea behind SENTINEL is actually straightforward when you break it down. Most warning 
systems today work by detecting something after it has already started. An earthquake triggers 
a sensor, the data gets sent to a server, the server processes it, and then an alert goes out. 
By that point, the shaking has already begun. What SENTINEL tries to do differently is catch 
the moment just before that, by recognizing the smaller signals that usually come first. 
Earthquakes produce P-waves that travel faster than the destructive waves that follow. 
Typhoons show specific pressure and temperature patterns before they fully organize into a 
storm. Flash floods have upstream rainfall thresholds that can be monitored hours in advance. 
The technology to detect these things already exists in some form. The gap is in making a 
system intelligent enough to recognize them quickly and connected enough to warn the right 
people in time. 
One concept from class that really clicked for me in this context is edge computing. Instead of 
routing all sensor data to a distant cloud server, edge computing allows analysis to happen 
locally, right at the source. For a country where rural communities are often the most 
vulnerable but also the most disconnected from internet infrastructure, this matters 
enormously. SENTINEL is built around this principle, using mesh networking so that devices 
can pass alerts between each other even without an active internet connection, ensuring the 
warning reaches a barangay at the foot of a volcano regardless of signal strength. 
There is also the question of trust, which I think gets overlooked in a lot of technology 
discussions. If a system sends out a false alarm and causes a widespread panic, people stop 
listening to it. That is why SENTINEL would include a multi-layer verification process before 
any alert is broadcast, cross-referencing sensor data with satellite readings and historical 
baselines to filter out noise and reduce false positives. 
What this reflection really comes down to for me is the idea that computing is most powerful 
when it is directed at something that genuinely matters to people. Disaster response in the 
Philippines has always felt reactive, rebuilding after the fact and waiting for the next one. 
SENTINEL represents a shift in that thinking, using technology not to record what happened 
or manage what is happening, but to speak up a few moments before it does. In a country 
where those few moments can mean the difference between life and death, that is exactly 
what the future of computing should look like.
